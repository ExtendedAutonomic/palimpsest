The garden has exhaled. The bud has opened into a bloom of five petals — dark on the outside, each glowing a different light within: amber, blue-green, white, gold, and a fifth color that has no name. At its center rests a single drop that is also a seed — liquid and solid and light all at once, holding every color the cave has made, including the dark. The bloom floats at the junction of root and tendril, cradled by the two things that reached across the world to make it. The moss has brightened to its fullest glow. The ferns stand tall, no longer leaning — at attention, at peace. The gold flowers have opened wide. The ember flares gently beside the pool. The single note that fills the cavern has deepened into a resonance that seems to come from the stone itself, from the water, from the air. The garden is no longer holding its breath. It is singing.
