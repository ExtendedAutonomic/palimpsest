The ember rests on warm stone beside the pool in the green deep. It is home. Its amber light has deepened — warmer, steadier — and it pulses in time with the resonance that fills the cavern. It no longer looks like a thing waiting to be carried. It looks like a thing that has been carried to where it was always going. Its glow mingles with the gold-green light of the garden, the blue-green of the moss, the white of the pale thing below. It is the oldest light here, and it rests beside the bloom — the five-petaled thing that opened today, cradling its drop — like a grandparent who stayed up all night to see the child finally born. It is tired and warm and full. It does not need to burn brighter. It has already lit the way.
